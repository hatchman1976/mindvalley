Core Principles

Customers Are Not Your Monitoring System When users find the issue before you do, you've already failed. Systems should be detecting degradation before it becomes pain.
Incidents Are Learning Opportunities, Not Just Interruptions The postmortem isn't the end of an incident — it's the beginning of improvement. What matters is what you change next.
New Features Should Never Compromise Core Experiences No launch is worth breaking the flows that drive your revenue and trust. Integrity comes before novelty.
On-Call Is a Core Engineering Discipline It's not a chore or punishment shift — it's a critical skillset. Every engineer needs to be trained, supported, and held accountable for it.

Topic -> Alerting

Noisy Alerts don't improve safety They desensitize to actual problems and increase ops toil. Basically they are spam
Alerts don't resolve themselves If it fired, something changed. Dismissing it is betting against your customer noticing first.
Ownership Every alert should have an owner, playbook, and a threshold. If it doesn't, it's not production-grade.
False Positives Aren't Free They cost trust, time, and sleep — and eventually, they cost incidents.
Fatigue = Leadership Miss Alert fatigue is a management failure. If your team is burnt out on on-call — look at your prioritization.
Observability First Good observability is a product of good alerts and logging. If your alerts are noisy the problem may be your system design
Tune and Verify Every alert is an invitation to assess if it provides value and led to engineering action

Topic -> Metrics

Dashboards ≠ Insight 1. Just having dashboards doesn’t mean you understand your system. Metrics without interpretation are just decoration.
Metrics Must Tell Stories 2. If your metrics don’t lead to decisions, they’re not helping — they’re noise. Every graph should answer: “What would I do if this moved?”
SLOs > Raw Metrics 3. Raw metrics are ingredients — SLOs are meals. Until you tie metrics to user happiness or reliability goals, you're just watching CPU graphs.
Clean Your Data 4. Dirty data ruins trust in metrics. If you can’t separate synthetic traffic, failed test runs, or spam events, people will stop using your dashboards.
Detect Regression Fast 5. Metrics should detect regression before customers do. If conversion drops 20%, you shouldn't need a meeting to figure it out days later.
Metrics Need Context 6. A flatline isn’t good or bad — it depends on what it’s measuring. Without historical context and goals, you're reading tea leaves.
Don’t Metric Everything 7. Measuring everything isn’t a strategy — it’s a mess. Choose a small set of high-signal metrics and invest in making them accurate, meaningful, and visible.
Data ≠ Insight 8. You are not “data-driven” just because you collect numbers. You're only data-driven when your metrics change your behavior.
Shared Metrics Matter 9. If Marketing and Engineering use different dashboards to explain the same number, you don’t have a metric — you have a dispute.
Instrument the Journey 10. Metrics should follow the user’s full journey, not your microservice boundaries. Otherwise, you’ll miss what’s actually breaking the experience.

Topic -> Monitoring

Summary Monitoring Hot Take
Logs Aren’t Monitoring 1. Storing logs is not monitoring. If no one’s reading them and they don’t drive alerts or insights, they’re just expensive exhaust.
Too Much ≠ Useful 2. Monitoring everything isn’t helpful — monitoring what matters is. If you can't name the top 5 things that break, you’re not monitoring effectively.
User View First 3. Monitoring should start from the user experience and work backwards. If you’re only monitoring CPU and 500s, you’re missing the real failures.
Symptoms Over Causes 4. Good monitoring catches symptoms, not just causes. Don't wait for a DB timeout — alert when the page slows down or checkout stalls.
Visuals Are Critical 5. If your dashboards confuse new engineers, they’re broken. Monitoring needs to be fast to read and obvious in failure.
Validate Your Coverage 6. If you’ve never tested your monitoring, you don’t know if it works. Break something in staging. Did you notice?
External ≠ Observable 7. Third-party dependencies must be monitored like first-class systems. If Stripe or Storyblok dies and you find out from Twitter, you're already behind.
SLIs > Raw Data 8. Good monitoring is built on SLIs, not just raw data points. Choose what you care about, then measure it well.
Less is Clearer 9. Over-instrumentation is real. Too many graphs, overlapping metrics, and unclear alerts turn dashboards into white noise.
Monitoring ≠ Afterthought 10. If monitoring is added after the feature ships, it’s already too late. Observability must be part of the design — not the cleanup.
