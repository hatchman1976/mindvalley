Core Principles

Customers Are Not Your Monitoring System When users find the issue before you do, you've already failed. Systems should be detecting degradation before it becomes pain.
Incidents Are Learning Opportunities, Not Just Interruptions The postmortem isn't the end of an incident — it's the beginning of improvement. What matters is what you change next.
New Features Should Never Compromise Core Experiences No launch is worth breaking the flows that drive your revenue and trust. Integrity comes before novelty.
On-Call Is a Core Engineering Discipline It's not a chore or punishment shift — it's a critical skillset. Every engineer needs to be trained, supported, and held accountable for it.

Topic -> Alerting

Summary Alerting Hot Take
Noisy Alerts Hurt 1. Noisy alerts don't make you safer — they just make you numb. If your engineers roll their eyes when PagerDuty goes off, your alert system is broken.
Actionless = Spam 2. If an alert doesn't lead to action, it's not an alert — it's spam. Be honest: are you paging people or just logging anxiety?
Investigate Everything Fired 3. Alerts don't resolve themselves. If it fired, something changed. Dismissing it without investigating is just betting against your customer noticing first.
Training via Alerts 4. Every alert trains your team — for better or worse. If most alerts are noise, you're teaching your engineers to ignore real signals.
Don't Hoard Alerts 5. "We've always had that alert" is not a reason to keep it. That's not alert hygiene — that's organizational hoarding.
Ownership is Required 6. Every alert should have a clear owner, a documented playbook, and a known threshold. If you can't name all three, it's not production-grade.
False Positives Cost 7. False positives aren't free. They cost trust, time, and sleep — and eventually, they cost incidents.
Fatigue = Leadership Miss 8. Alert fatigue is a management failure, not an individual one. If your team is burnt out on on-call, don't look at PagerDuty — look at your prioritization.
Observability First 9. Good alerting is a product of good observability and clear SLOs. If your alerts are noisy, the problem might not be the alert — it might be your system design.
Root Cause First 10. Every alert is an invitation to ask: "Should this even be possible?" Great teams don't just tune alerts — they eliminate the failure modes behind them.
